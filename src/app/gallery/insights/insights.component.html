The unique selling point

Visual art is core to who I am, and you’ll almost always find a me accompanied by a sketchpad of sorts which I’ll use to scribble on during downtime. I’m also the type of person who rarely leaves the house without a camera, because sometimes the best photographs are the more spontaneous ones.

The visual art I display on my site generally tends to be hobby work as opposed to anything professional, however at work I am tasked with bringing the best of these talents and skills into my day-to-day job.

Manifesto

There are four main goals I try to adhere to when I have my artist’s hat on:

  • Venture into the unknown. Never be afraid to experiment. Push the bounds of every toolset.
  • Challenge myself. Iteratively improve upon previous works by learning new techniques.
  • Contribute back. Create art not only for myself, but for the community as well.
  • Learn from others. Critique and provide constructive criticism that promotes growth.

On vector graphics

I create the bulk of my vector graphics using Inkscape. While I’ve used other vector editors in the past, I find myself returning to Inkscape, as it is the tool I have the most experience with.

My vector graphics can go from ultra-realistic to completely whimsical and absurd. For my personal images, the end result is usually dependent upon mood and the type of piece I’m creating: I’ve been known to create simplistic cartoons for months on end before switching to the opposite end of the spectrum and creating photorealistic still life images.

My professional vector works tend to primarily focus on visual design and UX wireframing, using tools such as Sketch and Balsamiq Mockups. I always make an effort to inject a bit of my personality into everything I create.

On photography

A lot of my photography is the correct mix of luck, lighting and determination. While I have a decent camera with a pretty bulky set of interchangeable lenses, I’ve always maintained that a good photographer can make any camera take an interesting photo.

When I started off working with photos, I worked with film. Obviously, this meant that I could only take a set number of photos before I had to pay for a new reel of film, but this has served as a great lesson on being efficient with photos I take.
